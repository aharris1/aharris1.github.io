Crater Recognition from Eigenvalue Expansion

I've developed a novel approach to recognizing craters with neural nets by leveraging an eigenvalue expansion to reduce the dimensionality of a digital terrain model by several orders of magnitude, enabling reduction of network complexity and minimization of distortions resulting from up- and down-sampling sections to match the input layer dimensions. Preliminary results have shown a recognition precision of 98.8%, recall of 95.4%, and an F1 score of 0.97 on a randomized test batch of 500 pre-identified craters and 500 random lunar tiles.

2016-2018 Independent Astronomy Research:
Crater Recognition Algorithm

From 2017 onwards I have been performing independent astronomy research to utilize a a neural net with a novel transformation layer for crater recognition. The crater recognition neural net I developed uses only 88 neurons, with a single hidden layer of 25 neurons. It has a recognition precision of 98.8%, recall of 95.4%, and an F1 score of 0.97 on a randomized test batch of 500 pre-identified craters and 500 random lunar tiles. To ensure that the metrics were not the result of overfitting, the test data was not shown to the network during training and the complexity of the neural net was minimized.

2016-2017 ASME Student Design Competition:
Robot Pentathlon

I proposed and oversaw the new competition robotics initiative through negotiating for a $5,000 budget, then forming and leading a team of 7 undergraduates to compete in the 2016 Student Design Competition: Robot Pentathlon. The project required the design and fabrication of a robot that fit within a 50cm cubic bounding box with the ability to drive in a straight line, launch a tennis ball, hit a golf ball, climb stairs, and lift a weight that was itself designed and fabricated by the team.

A novel electromagnetic torque transmission system was considered for the entry, and I performed a feasibility study for that concept using a test platform I designed. Upon analysis of the data it was clear that the empirical evidence did not suggest feasibility, and so I informed my team that we would have to redesign the robot around more conventional concepts. As a result of the study I performed, the team and I had sufficient time to perform the redesign and were able to compete well enough to be awarded a 6th of 80 ranking at the national competition.

2017-2018 RoboGames Heavyweight Combat Robot

Utilizing the momentum from the prior year and my newly-won position as President of the Stevens ASME Chapter, in 2017 I negotiated for a $26,000 budget and recruited a team of 70 undergraduates for a RoboGames Heavyweight Combat Robotics competition run.

Based on analysis of the current dominant designs in the competition, a novel three degree-of-freedom drive system was proposed and justified. A driving simulator was produced to provide training for the robot operators and feedback for the drive system designers. I derived analytical dynamics models from first-principles, which were used to produce a set of quantified specifications for the design of the subsystems. Quantifying the specifications early allowed the effective integration of subsystems and delegation of the design responsibility for those subsystems. To further enable effective delegation, I produced LaTeX documentation for my analytical models so they could be understood, extended, and utilized by the subteams as necessary to ensure they met the specifications I originally laid out.

Furthermore, when difficult analysis problems arose during the course of the project, I would take responsibility for ensuring that they were approached from an analysis-first perspective and developed models when necessary to keep the project on schedule.

Ultimately, while I was able to arrange for getting the team, robot, and associated 1000 pound crate to the competition in California, an electronics failure prevented the robot from being able to enter the ring. During the final week leading up to the competition, I worked with the electronics subteam to troubleshoot the cause of the electrical system failures. Unfortunately, those failures were not able to be rectified in time, and so in the interest of preserving the mechanical subsystems for future teams to work on, the robot was not permitted to enter the arena.
