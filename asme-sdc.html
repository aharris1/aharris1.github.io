2017 ASME Student Design Competition

Robot Pentathlon

Project Successes

  • Took 6th place out of 80 competitors.
  • Fabricated robot without regular access to machining equipment or shop space.
  • Competed in all 5 events.
  • Travelled from NYC to Tennessee.
  • Recruited skilled team, effectively delegated technical work and supervised integration thereof.
  • Experimentally replicated PhD thesis work on eddy-current braking for feasibility analysis.
    • Experimentally determined that the contactless electromagnetic transmission concept was infeasible using a scale model.
    • Results provided team with sufficient time to redesign before competition.
    • Gay, S. E. (2005). Contactless magnetic brake for automotive applications. ProQuest Dissertations and Theses.

Project Failures

  • The robot had to be completed in the hotel room the night before the competition.
  • No margin was left between the edge of the treads and the edge of the steps, leading to the robot being unable to descend the stairs in the stair climbing challenge.

Lessons Learned and Skills Developed

  • Grant and Sponsorship Acquisition - I negotiated for the budget for this project, including working directly with Stevens' committee for providing grants for student organizations and part manufacturers. Those partnerships led to the acquisition of $5000 in direct support from the institution and an equivalent of $1000 of in-kind donations from Intralox, the manufacturer of the treads used.
  • Machining Techniques and Limitations - The test apparatus used to perform experimental feasibility analysis of the contactless electromagnetic transmission was machined out of aluminum and brass, which provided me with the opportunity to solicit advice and instruction from the Stevens machining staff. Processes involved include lathing, vertical milling, and bandsaw cutting, with emphasis on appropriate applications and the resulting limitations of the process.
  • Part Procurement - The 5 different events required for the competition required finding sources for required components including electromechanical actuators, batteries, radio controllers, electronic speed controllers, and mechanical parts.
  • Leadership and Team Management - The team was composed of volunteers, and so I had to ensure that I maintained team morale even when the project was challenging and the time required became intimidating. Furthermore, I had to ensure that appropriate external partnerships were fostered within Stevens Institute of Technology, including forging a close partnership with the director of the Mechanical Engineering Department, such that travel and lodging accommodations were provided. To express gratitude and to help solidify those partnerships for future projects, I coordinated with the team to provide team-signed thank you cards to external partners who had provided instrumental support for the project.
  • Experimental Feasibility Analysis - Modeling eddy-current electromagnetic torque transmission in COMSOL produces encouraging results, but the complexity of the phenomena required empirical validation to ensure that the concept was feasible before I committed significant time and resources to a design based around that novel concept. The experimental procedure, results, and images of the experimental apparatus is shown below in additional media.
  • Analytical Dynamic Modeling - The stair-climbing event required making sure that the center-of-gravity of the robot was placed such that the robot would tip forward onto the next step instead of rolling over backwards. To help my team ensure that requirement was met, I developed a dynamic analytical model in the geometry software GeoGebra, which is embedded in the additional media section below. It represents a dynamic free body model to help visualize the gravitational force vector and the rear support point as the robot ascends the step. If the gravitational vector crosses the step before it crosses the rear support point E of the chassis, represented by line EF then it shows that the robot with that center of gravity will tip forward onto the step during the climb. However, if the gravitational vector crosses behind the rear support point before the edge of the step, then the robot will tumble backwards during the climb.
